Key Findings

What the data
actually shows

Six findings, ordered by importance. The core result is consistent: infrastructure access is the primary lever. Demographics reflect need, not supply.

Suppressed demand is not the same as absent demand. The gap between predicted and actual ridership is an infrastructure problem — and an investment opportunity.

Findings

01
Accessibility

Distance and density outperform simple stop counts

The most predictive variables are not raw infrastructure counts, but how close and how dense transit service is. dist_to_bus, dist_to_rail, and bus_density_2mi consistently dominate both the OLS and Random Forest models. bus_density_2mi alone accounts for approximately 58% of total Random Forest feature importance — more than all other variables combined. A tract with many stops far away underperforms a tract with fewer stops that are nearby and walkable.

02
Model Result

Bus density within 2 miles is the single strongest predictor

Across both models, bus_density_2mi ranks as the top feature by importance and significance. Tracts in the top quartile of bus density average 3–4× the transit share of tracts in the bottom quartile. This is not a marginal effect — it reflects a structural difference in how accessible the network is in practice. Service needs to be abundant within walking and cycling range, not just present somewhere in the vicinity.

03
Accessibility

Distance is a barrier — and there's a threshold

The relationship between distance and ridership is not linear. Beyond approximately 1 mile from the nearest bus stop, transit share drops sharply — a threshold effect that Random Forest captures and OLS misses. Tracts past the 1-mile mark show near-zero ridership regardless of other favorable conditions. This has a direct implication for service design: closing access gaps in the 0.5–1.5 mile range produces far greater ridership returns than expanding service in already well-served areas.

04
Model Result

Demographics indicate demand — they cannot create access

Socioeconomic variables (income, race, foreign-born share) are statistically significant but secondary. All demographic features combined contribute less than 20% of Random Forest feature importance; no single variable exceeds 8%. They reflect who needs transit, not whether transit is available. High-need communities with poor infrastructure still underperform on ridership. This reframes the equity argument: demographic targeting identifies where to look, but supply-side investment is the actual lever.

05
Spatial Gap

Suppressed demand clusters in identifiable places

By comparing predicted transit share (from the Random Forest model) to actual observed ridership, the analysis surfaces tracts with significant positive residuals — places where the model expects high usage but observes low usage. These are not low-demand areas; they are infrastructure-constrained areas. They are not randomly distributed: they concentrate in middle-ring suburbs with fragmented bus networks — particularly in Bergen, Morris, Somerset, and Middlesex counties — where demand exists but the network has not kept up. The gap map is the most actionable output of this project.

06
Policy Implication

Targeted investment outperforms blanket expansion

Scenario modeling confirms the core finding: a uniform +20% bus stop increase (Scenario 1) distributes modest gains across already-served areas. Concentrating that same investment in high-predicted-demand, low-access tracts (Scenario 2) produces substantially higher average ridership gains — because it targets tracts near or past the 1-mile distance threshold, where new access directly converts to new ridership. The optimal strategy is a hybrid: targeted bus expansion (Scenario 2) first, followed by rail improvements (Scenario 3) in the corridors where a denser bus network already brings riders to stations.

Summary

The Central Argument

Where transit is close and dense, people ride it. Where it isn't, they don't — not because they don't want to, but because it isn't there.

The data is unambiguous: bus stop density within 2 miles (58% of RF feature importance) and distance to the nearest stop explain far more variance in ridership than income, race, or age — individually or combined. The planning implication is direct: investment decisions are behavior-shaping decisions. Demographic maps reveal who needs transit; the gap map reveals where to build. Prioritize the gap zones. Target the areas where infrastructure is the binding constraint, not the absence of demand.
