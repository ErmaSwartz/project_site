A spatial and machine learning analysis of transit performance across Northern New Jersey — identifying infrastructure gaps, modeling suppressed demand, and simulating targeted investments.

Transit usage is driven more by accessibility — distance and density — than by demographics alone. Investment decisions directly shape behavior.

Project Overview

01 — What This Is

Infrastructure access, not demographics, determines ridership

This project combines spatial analysis, econometric modeling (OLS), and machine learning (Random Forest) to identify where transit demand exists, where access is lacking, and where targeted investments would have the greatest impact across ~2,181 census tracts in Northern New Jersey.

02 — Key Innovation

Accessibility metrics, not stop counts

Distance to nearest stop and stops within 2 miles — the two variables that explain more variance than all demographic factors combined. How close and how dense matters more than whether a stop exists.

03 — What Makes This Different

Three engineered features drive the entire model

dist_to_bus, dist_to_rail, and bus_density_2mi — computed via spatial joins in NJ State Plane coordinates — account for ~80% of Random Forest feature importance. Demographic variables (income, race, nativity) are included but remain secondary. The analysis separates who needs transit from whether transit exists nearby.

Key Findings at a Glance

Finding 01

Accessibility drives transit use

Distance and density outperform simple infrastructure counts. bus_density_2mi alone accounts for 58% of Random Forest feature importance — more than all demographic variables combined.

Finding 02

Spatial gaps are the opportunity

Tracts with positive predicted-minus-actual gaps are not low-demand areas — they are access-constrained areas. The gap map surfaces the investment priority queue directly from model output.

Finding 03

There's a 1-mile threshold

Beyond ~1 mile from the nearest bus stop, transit share drops sharply regardless of other variables. This nonlinear effect — missed by OLS — is exactly what Random Forest captures and what targeted investment exploits.

Finding 04

Targeted beats blanket expansion

Scenario 2 (targeted bus in high-gap tracts) produces higher average ridership gains than a system-wide +20% expansion — with fewer infrastructure units. Demographics identify who needs transit; the model identifies where to build it.

What This Demonstrates

Portfolio Signal

GIS + data science + policy reasoning, integrated end-to-end

This project combines spatial data engineering (GeoPandas, EPSG:3424 joins), econometric modeling (OLS, statsmodels), machine learning (Random Forest, scikit-learn), and scenario simulation — then translates all of it into maps and findings a planning audience can use. The goal is not to demonstrate every technique, but to surface the insight that drives better investment decisions.
